# Editing — visuals, continuity, and immersive audio

> Editing is far more than cutting dead space. A great editing style can win
> perfect watch time and millions of views; the wrong one can bury a great video.
> **Start from the experience your viewers want**, then execute the pillars below.

## Match the style to the desired experience (before any technique)

- **Sam** (a guy in his car, ~90s between cuts, 17 cuts in 26 min) works because
  his viewers want to feel like they're *hanging out with someone.* Flashy quick
  cuts would **destroy** that authentic experience — and disrupting what the
  viewer came for is the best way to make them leave.
- **Mr Beast** (rapid-fire) works because his audience came for *entertainment.*
- **Cutting out all your pauses** so you speak constantly is the single most
  important edit for *entertainment* — but it costs *authenticity.* Every editing
  choice is a trade-off along that axis; pick to fit your audience.

Learn how the **best creators in your niche** edit, then build something similar.

## Pillar 1 — A healthy variety of visuals

A change in your field of vision grabs attention; a minute with only 3 things to
look at is boring. Switch footage types every few seconds — **but** over-cutting
becomes "visual mush" and confusion (see the drawback rule below).

**Footage types:**
- **A-roll** — you're seen *and* heard at once. Personal, holds attention well.
  Use for **short** bursts when saying something important; zoom in on your face
  to signal "listen up."
- **B-roll** — footage separate from the audio that *shows* what you're saying.
  Clearer and faster than talking-head; **use as much as possible.**
- **Stock footage** — the lazy version of b-roll.
- **Motion graphics / animation** — explains complicated things crystal-clear and
  fast (a Dude Perfect math puzzle explained instantly by animation where a-roll
  or normal b-roll would be too slow or confusing).

**The drawback rule:** don't minimize shot duration just to minimize it. Leaving
one clip on screen for 10 seconds is fine *if it's interesting for 10 seconds.*
Over-cutting → the viewer can't process anything → engagement drops.

**Add engagement without cutting to a new clip:**
- Give **movement to still clips** — slowly change scale or position; better, change
  *perspective*. Use abrupt scale changes (zoom) to draw the eye to something. (Wiggle
  keyframes exist too; optional/stylistic.)
- **Captions:** only to make the viewer pay closer attention to certain words —
  not to fill dead space (that's obnoxious). Keep to **3 words or fewer** at a
  time so they're easy to read. Better than nothing if there's truly nothing else.
- **Guide attention to the focus point of an image** (plainly showing the whole
  image is boring *and* confusing). Six ways:
  1. Animate the most important text / highlighted area.
  2. Darken or blur everything around the focus point.
  3. Shift the hue — **red = negative** connotation; **yellow/green = positive.**
  4. Circles, arrows, underlines.
  5. Make the subject **glow.**
  6. (Baseline) scale and position toward it.
- **Make the video look good** overall — color grading, a vignette, subtle particle
  animations.

## Pillar 2 — Visual continuity (invisible editing, mesmerizing experience)

A seamless feed of visuals leaves no rough edges where distraction builds and no
point where immersion breaks. **Goal: every moment blends into the next; the
editing is invisible, the experience mesmerizing.**

- A graphic should never just *pop* into existence — **animate it into frame**, or
  let it appear together with a **sound effect** (a shutter, a pop) that explains
  its arrival.
- **The cut rule:** keep the viewer's focus point consistent across a cut. If
  their eyes are on your face and the next frame forces them to the far side of
  the screen, it's jarring and breaks immersion. Don't make the eye jump sides
  every cut.
- Use **full-screen transitions** to make cuts seamless.
- **Deliberately break continuity now and then** — a hard jolt grabs attention.

## Pillar 3 — Immersive audio & music (the "digital morphine")

Sound design lets the viewer experience every cut, transition, animation, and clip
through a **second sense** — literally double the stimulation, and a lever on the
viewer's mood (an engagement superpower).

- **Gather a sound library** (a free SFX pack, freesound.org, or a licensed
  platform like Epidemic Sound). Go through the video and add SFX wherever it makes
  sense — a **whoosh** on movement, a **highlight** sound on a highlight.
- **Three emotion-hijacking SFX** (don't overdo any — they lose their power):
  - **Risers** — build tension/anticipation; signal something important is coming.
    *Only* use when something important actually happens, or they lose credibility.
  - **Hits** — release tension and emphasize a moment as important (optionally put a
    riser before a hit).
  - **Drones** — mystery / intrigue / underlying suspense; reserved for darker moods.
- **Music choice** carries huge emotional weight:
  - Pick songs by the **mood the viewer should feel** for each subject (an
    "anticipation" song makes them feel you're about to say something important; an
    "innovative" song makes advice feel exciting). Split the video by subject change,
    then assign a mood per segment. Spend real time here — fit matters a lot.
  - **Sync the song's hits/dips to the video** — line the music's pickup to the
    exact moment you move from problem → solution to mark the shift and add energy.
  - **Pause the music abruptly** to jolt attention and spotlight a special moment.
  - **Fade the music out** to signal a segment is ending → creates anticipation of
    something new.
  - Stems: downloading a song's **separate elements** (an Epidemic Sound feature)
    lets you layer/adjust tracks independently.

## Pacing (the core of editing)

Pacing must be **as fast as the content allows.** Benchmark: even a slow 90-minute
nature documentary cut 3 clips in 15 seconds (~5s/clip) with a minimalist style —
so a talking-head lingering 10–20s per clip is far too slow. Cut the pauses where
you figure out what to say next, and any dead couple-seconds where nothing happens.

**Vary the energy** — the most underrated tip: a hyper-edited section abruptly
dropping into a mellow a-roll segment *forces* the viewer to re-focus. These
pacing changes keep them from zoning out.
