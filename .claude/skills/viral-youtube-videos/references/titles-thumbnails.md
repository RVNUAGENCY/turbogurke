# Titles & thumbnails — winning the click (CTR)

> Goal: create **as many captivating reasons to watch as possible.** People
> notice your title & thumbnail *before* anyone else's is the single most pivotal
> advantage a video can have — so getting title+thumbnail right is the most
> important thing a YouTuber does. These tactics also double as the **criteria
> for a good idea**: if you can't apply them to an idea, don't make the video.

## First principles

- **A reason to watch must be *immediately* apparent — under 1 second.** If it
  takes longer than a second to think of a reason, or the reason is far-fetched,
  the title/thumbnail is weak.
- **Title and thumbnail must BUILD on each other, never repeat.** Repetition
  ("airplane facts" in both) delivers info at a snail's pace while the 10 other
  videos next to yours explain themselves faster. Use *both* slots to add
  *different* intrigue → stack multiple questions in the viewer's mind.
- **The thumbnail's first job is to get *looked at* (a "spotlight element"),** its
  second is to intrigue. Aim to make the thumbnail convincing enough to click
  *without even reading the title.*
- **Make thumbnails from scratch, not from the video.** Don't screenshot. Shoot
  new photos / composite images. Thumbnails *represent* the video, they aren't
  literal (a face, a pacifier, a plane + basketballs that could never be photographed for real).
- **Clarity is the foundation.** Any confusion washes out every other tactic.
  Clarity = "I know what's happening" (required). Uncertainty = "I don't know
  what happens *next*" (good). Confusion = "I don't know what's happening" (fatal).
- **Don't describe the whole video — communicate the 1–2 most intriguing parts.**
  A 40-min philosophy video opens on the single broadest-appeal hook, *then*
  introduces more hooks. Communicate *what makes it interesting*, not *what it is*.

## The three spotlight elements (make the thumbnail get noticed)

Incorporate at least one; it must pop from the whole YouTube page, not just the image:
1. **A face** (expressive) — eyes are magnetic; we even see faces where there are none.
2. **A familiar object** — recognition bias; do something *weird* with it to spark questions.
3. **A short piece of text** — very short; it grabs, it doesn't explain.

Plus: **an art style unique in your niche** stands out *and* signals a
higher-quality video (Lemmino-style artistic thumbnails build trust; deliberately
crude ones also stand out). Use the *extra* room in the thumbnail for subtle
storytelling — posture, setting, lighting, color, small text ("Day 2", "41
minutes in") — to promise an experience better than any title could.

## Titling

- **Don't title too literally.** "Spending 45 minutes in the world's quietest
  room" is boring; make it dramatic and curiosity-driving. **Title the video as
  the *driving question*** that leads *into* the video.
- **Sometimes you *must* be literal** to avoid confusion or clickbait feel — then
  keep it clear.
- **Most important / most interesting words FIRST.** Never bury the key word at
  the end where it gets cut off or missed.
- **Short, common words with a very clear meaning.** Avoid long titles.
- **Tools:** thesaurus.com and ChatGPT for sharper word choice (the difference
  between "I went to" and "I tested one-star theme parks" is deliberate word-hunting).

## The 26 title & thumbnail tactics

Each applies to most video types. Master all; find ways to apply several per video.

1. **Build the thumbnail from scratch** — represent the video, don't restrict it to literal footage (lets you add a face, an object, an impossible shot).
2. **Depict something alarming** — attention is biased toward danger (police + a scared face).
3. **Look special / novel** — a unique art style implies unique information; makes a "you'll learn something new" promise believable.
4. **Maximize brightness contrast** — subject must not blend into the background (dark puffer vs light; white vs dark background).
5. **Show something/someone familiar** — recognition bias; a recognizable emoji/face/brand gets your thumbnail seen *first*, before the competition (this is the most pivotal advantage — 8 of these tactics serve it).
6. **Suggest the viewer is (or risks) making a mistake / danger** — "If they do this, you're being manipulated." Create FOMO; the antidote is watching.
7. **Communicate the 1–2 most intriguing parts, not the whole video** — open on the broadest, most intriguing hook; add more hooks inside.
8. **Thumbnail intrigues *before* the title is read** — show the most interesting thing so it works pre-title.
9. **Make it 1-second simple** — a cluttered thumbnail hides every element; on mobile the video auto-plays after ~1s of no scroll, so you literally have 1 second.
10. **Maximize color contrast** — every color/saturation/brightness choice is deliberate (a bright orange coat is not an accident).
11. **Suggest someone is hiding a secret** — "What Sam Altman doesn't want you to know," "Who is he *really*?" Use secrets/uncommon knowledge you have.
12. **Suggest you'll help them reach a goal / ease a pain point** — bonus if you also imply they're currently doing it wrong.
13. **Give an accurate *stylistic* impression** — for unknown/saturated topics, a highly stylized artistic thumbnail creates trust that it's a good watch.
14. **Be perfectly clear — never confusing** — keep it simple ("the problem with this humanoid robot"); clarity is the foundation for every other tactic working.
15. **Develop a mystery / sense of wonder** — thought-provoking question ("Is your red the same as my red?"), or "why are these theme parks rated 1 star?"; give something *tangible* to wonder about ("Don't try the yellow slide").
16. **Frame the ordinary as extraordinary** — word choice matters ("I *tested* one-star theme parks" beat the first phrasing the creator thought of).
17. **Promise a spectacle (FOMO)** — a hilariously overpowered Beyblade; add elements (torn mannequins, "speed of sound" text) that raise expectations of what happens.
18. **Develop a puzzle with an obviously missing piece** — "I guess it was a scam all along." Works *only* when the missing piece feels *important* (pair it with something alarming/out-of-control).
19. **Challenge beliefs / suggest they're misinformed** — "the reason you don't trust tap water," "maybe there's only one game of tic-tac-toe" (challenged a common belief → ~400× the creator's normal views).
20. **Leverage a trusted brand** (yours or someone else's) — a known face/character (Alex Hormozi's face; Tommy Shelby) carries recognition *and* trust into the video.
21. **Share proof you're worth the time** — back a bold promise with evidence ("fall asleep in 2 minutes — *military technique*"; credentials that say why she's worth listening to).
22. **Introduce an unsolved problem** — "Frogs keep drowning themselves in my pool" — sad + surprising → you want to see it solved.
23. **Share something surprising / counterintuitive** — "why farmers can't legally replant their own seeds."
24. **Include a reactive facial expression** — shock and fear work best because they're obviously a reaction to something extraordinary; you crave to know what they're reacting to (and they set emotional expectations).
25. **Show a situation with an unknown before/after** — an alarming scene (Ryan + someone in the window) that makes you wonder what led to / follows it.
26. **Make people think "there's no freaking way"** — do something extraordinary (Mr Beast did this broke, with plastic knives cutting a table). You don't need money — you need something extraordinary.

## Applying it (coaching moves)

- For any idea, ask: **what are the 1–2 most intriguing parts, and which tactics
  turn them into reasons to click?** If you can't find any, the idea is weak.
- Check **title vs thumbnail aren't repeating** — each should add a *new* question.
- Count the **reasons to watch** the pair creates. More stacked questions = more
  robust against the 10 competing videos.
- Verify **clarity** first, then push **uncertainty/intrigue**.
- The intro must then *immediately deliver* the promised content **and feeling** →
  see `hooks-intros.md`.
