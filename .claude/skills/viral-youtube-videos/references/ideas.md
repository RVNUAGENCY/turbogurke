# Video ideas — the 3-part idea engine

> A good idea can pull triple the views with half the work. Messing up the idea
> undermines the entire video, which makes this the single most important step.
> Ideas must be **broad** — attractive to *most* people in your niche, not one
> narrow feature. One video in a *popular area of interest* can beat a thousand
> videos in an *unpopular* one.

The trap to avoid: making a video about *one small feature / narrow topic* only
a sliver of your niche cares about. Aim for the **bullseye of a popular area of
interest** — the craziest / most bizarre / most broadly wanted thing.

---

## Part 1 — Find what your audience actually wants

Build one big list of the videos your target audience watches most, then read it
for patterns. This reveals your audience's *criteria for a great idea.*

1. **Pull your audience's viewing.** YouTube Studio → **Analytics → Audience**.
   Scroll to **"Videos your audience watches"** and **"Channels your audience
   watches."**
2. **Expand to your *potential* audience,** not just current. Add other channels
   your audience would like; open videos and grab channels from the *Suggested*
   column. Spend at least an hour — you want essentially every video your target
   audience watches in one place.
3. **Grab the top ~15 most-viewed videos per channel.** No need to be exhaustive
   per channel; the top viewed are what a decent chunk of the audience has seen.
4. **Sort by most-viewed and hunt for patterns.** Go deeper than topics — find
   *why* they got the interest:
   - Recurring **topics** and **formats** (e.g. "case studies of top creators").
   - **Pain points, goals, fears, insecurities**, subjects.
   - Does a **positive or negative** angle work better?
   - Which **words** demand attention? Which **concepts** does the niche love?
     (e.g. "play the game with a twist," chaotic thumbnails, question-based
     videos.)
5. **Write it all down** as a research checklist. You'll feed it into
   brainstorming next. Expect to feel stuck at first — that's normal.

A tool like **Velio / vidIQ** can automate finding these outliers, but the
manual version above works.

---

## Part 2 — Generate a surplus of ideas

Two strategies. Most of Leo's ideas come from #1.

### Strategy A — Be more creative (combinatorial)
Every new idea is old parts recombined (Gutenberg combined known components into
the printing press). So load up on components and let them combine:

- The components are: **the concepts your audience loves, the subjects they care
  about, and their pain points / wants / needs.** Research everything related to
  each; go down every rabbit hole; look for things that haven't been seen before.
- **More knowledge + more diverse knowledge = more unique ideas.**
- **Homework:** keep a notebook of research notes; **review it every single day**
  so the material is always simmering in the back of your mind.
- **You can't force creativity — you let it happen.** Don't sit and grind; let
  thoughts come. Science says *half-asleep* is the creative sweet spot:
  - Set the alarm a bit early → on wake, hit snooze → skim your notes ~2 min →
    fall back asleep → repeat each snooze. Ideas surface in that drowsy state.

### Strategy B — Borrow proven concepts, swap the subject (outliers)
Look **outside your niche** for a **concept** that massively outperformed its
channel's average — that's an **outlier video / outlier score**. Then apply the
concept to *your* audience's subject matter so it feels fresh.

- **The big mistake:** assuming any outlier concept will work for your audience.
  It won't. The concept must be **compatible** with what your audience wants.
  - Example: for a channel that helps YouTubers improve, `"[X] explained"` or
    `"[X] stereotypes"` are entertaining but *don't help* — so they're wrong.
    `"21 [X] lessons for [Y]"` and `"how to get so [X] you [Y]"` are *helpful* —
    so they fit.
- **Process:** find the phrases your audience is interested in (from Part 1),
  search them on an outlier tool (Velio/vidIQ), filter to **high views + high
  outlier score**, save good concepts to an ideas folder with a note on *how
  you'd use it* and *which specific parts you like.* Save the channels too, so
  you can browse a list of compatible-audience channels sorted by outlier score.

## Part 3 — The idea checklist (kill most of your ideas)

Only make the **top ~10%** of ideas. Raise the bar. In order:

1. **Fresh-perspective test (kills the most).** Write the idea down, wait a
   couple of days, re-read it. Half or more of the ideas that felt genius will
   now feel weak. Keep only the ones that still excite you.
2. **Has someone beaten you to it?** Search the would-be title + a few
   variations. If it exists, you can still win *if* you can improve the
   title/thumbnail or add a genuine twist (most videos have <10% CTR, so there's
   room). If you can't improve on it, drop it.
3. **Is it the *best* option you have?** Don't make a video on just any good
   idea — that wastes the effort. Aim for **5–10× outlier** ideas, not 1–2×.
4. **Make the title & thumbnail now, before anything else.** If you sat face to
   face with your viewer, why would they watch? There must be a solid connection
   between the **audience's motivation** (their interests, goals, pain points)
   and the **value this video provides**, and the title+thumbnail must make that
   connection instantly clear. **If you can't make that connection, the idea
   won't work — discard it.** (Then go to `titles-thumbnails.md`.)
