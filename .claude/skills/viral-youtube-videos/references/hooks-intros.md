# Hooks & intros — the first 5–30 seconds

> ~90% of viewership graphs have a sharp drop at the very beginning. The cause is
> almost always the same: the title/thumbnail got them curious, but the intro
> didn't give what they expected, so they left to find something better. The
> average video loses **over a third of its audience in the first 30 seconds.**

## The root fix: make the intro deliver on the title & thumbnail

**Write the title & thumbnail *and* the intro together.** Once you have the
title+thumbnail, ask: *What reason to watch did I just give? What expectations
did I create?* Then build an intro that **immediately lives up to them.**

Deliver on **two** things the title/thumbnail promised, not one:
1. **The content** — if you promised a train crash, *show the train crash* early.
2. **The feeling** — the pacing, mood, and style implied by the thumbnail. A
   Mr-Beast-style thumbnail promises **action**, so open fast (he spits ~5 words
   in the first second, with rush-in effects + train-horn SFX, and confirms the
   promised thing happens within ~5 seconds). A calm/comedic thumbnail promises
   something lighter — match *that* instead.

**Rule: create your intro at the pacing appropriate for the genre, and reflect
the style shown in the thumbnail.** A pool-robot video can spend 22 seconds on
the problem and still hold 10M views — because it opens on the problem
immediately *and* keeps things light, exactly as promised.

## Techniques that hook

- **Fast delivery draws people in.** Get to the promised content quickly; every
  sentence in an intro must be as meaningful as possible. Leo rewrites intros
  **up to 5 times** hunting the best adjectives, sentence structure, level of
  detail, and *order* of information.
- **Voiceovers are an intro superpower** — they let you deliver strong audio *and*
  strong visuals at the same time, and cut the camera every ~4 seconds for
  constant visual engagement, instead of sitting and talking at the lens.
- **Tell it as a story, not a description.** Introduce the elements, *then* reveal
  the turn ("this is a frog… it's dead… they keep drowning themselves and I don't
  know why"). Storytelling ordering is more engaging even when it takes longer.
- **Editing carries the intro as much as the script.** Strip the graphics, SFX,
  captions, transitions, and pick dull music and the *same* intro falls flat. Add
  a couple of these engagement elements every few seconds (genre-appropriate),
  with real attention to sound design and fresh visuals to look at.

## Then EXCEED expectations (don't just meet them)

Meeting expectations stops the drop; **exceeding** them raises the bar for the
whole video and gives the viewer confidence it's worth their time.

- **Know your audience:** who clicked, and what *else* would they want to know or
  see? In the intro, signal the video offers **more value than the title stated.**
- This may mean **not** wrapping the whole video into one tidy title sentence —
  that leaves nothing to surprise them with. Describe the coolest part, not
  everything.
- **Tease the highlight.** After delivering the title's promise in the first ~5
  seconds, Mr Beast adds "…we're also crashing countless cars… the most insane
  experiments of all time, starting with filling a house with 100,000 fireworks."
  He almost always stacks a bigger promise right after the first payoff.

## Diagnosing a weak intro (coaching checklist)

1. **Does it deliver the exact content the title/thumbnail promised, fast?**
2. **Does it deliver the promised *feeling* (pacing/mood/style)?**
3. **Is there a clear reason-to-watch established before any payoff?**
4. **Is every sentence pulling weight?** (If not, rewrite — up to 5 passes.)
5. **Do the visuals/SFX/music change every few seconds, or is it a talking head?**
6. **Does it *exceed* — tease extra value / the coolest part — or only meet?**
7. **Zero begging.** No "like, subscribe, hit the bell" — that's not why they came.

Retention tactics from `retention.md` also apply here — several of the strongest
hooks (mystery, stakes, a time limit, something extraordinary up front) *begin in
the intro.*
