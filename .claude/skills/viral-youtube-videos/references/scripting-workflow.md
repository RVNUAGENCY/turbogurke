# The full workflow — idea → upload

The end-to-end pipeline, with the scripting model at its center. Success on
YouTube = **make videos people click on and watch, done better than everyone
else.** The 7 steps below are how.

## Step 1 — Break the algorithm (mindset)
The algorithm just reflects what people want: if people click and watch, it
promotes the video more. That's all it does. So stop looking for tricks — make
something clickable and watchable, better than the competition.

## Step 2 — A good idea
Messing up the idea undermines everything, so this is the most important step.
- List everything interesting to your niche — what they **care about, are scared
  of, are insecure about.** Ask: *what titles can I craft that address my
  audience's needs?* (Figure out the content that lives up to the title later.)
- **The idea must be broad** — attractive to most of the niche, not one narrow
  feature. (Full engine in `ideas.md`.)
- Shortcut: study trends & popular videos in your niche, then **take them a step
  further or add a twist.**

## Step 3 — Title & thumbnail (do this *before* making the video)
This is how you **calculate the idea's quality.** There must be **at least one
very compelling reason to click.** If you can't create one, the idea sucks —
don't use it.
- Thumbnail: shoot **new photos** (often composited) that best represent the
  idea; don't use a raw frame/screenshot — it's bland and confusing next to the
  others. Most viewers read the thumbnail **before** the title.
- Title: a **question** is Leo's default reason-to-watch; or give extra context —
  just keep the reason to watch clear. (Full tactics in `titles-thumbnails.md`.)

## Step 4 — Plan the script (the water-tank model)
Think of retention as a **water tank with a leak in the bottom.** You start full,
it drains fast, and when it's empty viewers leave to find something better. You
**refill** by giving value — say/do something interesting, important, helpful, or
even just make them smile.
- The tank **drains at different speeds** by audience: some niches/ages have a
  ~20-second attention span, others 2–3 minutes. Know yours.
- **Spread key points evenly** through the video to keep refilling the tank.
- Structure: split the video into **sections**, each with a couple of bullet-point
  takeaways. Then **plan "pockets of dopamine"** — little edits, montages, or jokes
  that give a quick break. Without them the video flies by too fast and the viewer
  can't absorb anything.

## Step 5 — Write the script
- **Compact beats bloated.** A 10-min video that should be 3 min gets ~1-min
  average view duration; a 3-min video so compact it *feels* like 10 gets ~2.5-min
  ADV. Explain takeaways as simply as you can.
- **But don't over-trim** — cutting too far makes it chaotic and soulless. Get to
  the point quickly *while* keeping the spirit of a natural conversation. Make it
  feel like you're talking *to* the viewer, who's an engaged listener thinking and
  asking questions.
- **Vary the delivery** so it isn't monotonous — switch between analogy, example,
  story, and question.
- **"Valuable" tangents (good fluff):** to reach length without padding, *add*
  value rather than stretch what's there. Ryan Trahan extended a 5-min idea to
  exactly 8 minutes with small, funny, personal asides that each **added** something
  (humor, a personal connection that makes you stick around) — not by dragging out
  the existing content. Fluff that *adds* is fine; fluff that *stretches* is not.
- **The intro is the most important part of the script.** Align its **pacing,
  mood, and content** with the title & thumbnail, confirm the video will be what
  they expected, then immediately start delivering. (Full detail in
  `hooks-intros.md`.) **Never** ask for likes/subs/money — that's not why they came.
- **Binge-ability — "there is no such thing as an outro."** Don't half-ass the
  second half counting on people to stay; keep it as strong as the first so they
  roll into *another* of your videos. Think of your channel 5–10 videos at a time,
  not one — bingeing is the strongest signal you can give YouTube. Cut credits/outros.
- **AI:** fine for vocabulary, shortening sentences, improving flow — **never** to
  write the script. AI is trained on books/websites and writes like them, but
  YouTube isn't written like a book.
- **Don't stall for perfection.** Writing is a process; it's never perfect on the
  first pass. The worst mistake is writing nothing while chasing perfect.

## Step 6 — Recording
Gear barely matters (Leo's first two videos: iPhone + Blue Snowball, and they did
great). What matters is getting **many clips** — you can't have one continuous
talking shot; the viewer needs variety to look at. Leo would record ~10 hours of
footage just to switch clips every ~5 seconds. The attention impact is massive.

## Step 7 — Editing
Pacing is the core — **as fast as possible** — and the style must match your genre
and audience. Vary the energy (hyper-edited → mellow a-roll) to force re-focus, and
use sound design and a variety of music. (Full detail in `editing.md`.)
