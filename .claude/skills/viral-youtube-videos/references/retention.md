# Retention — keeping the watch

> The whole of audience retention in one sentence:
> **Maintain constant clarity in the viewer's mind as to what they'll miss out on
> if they stop watching.**

Every tactic below is a way to keep that "what you'd miss" alive. The best
creators have mastered all of them. Don't expect to apply all 18 to one video —
keep the list handy and reach for the ones that fit.

## The 18 retention tactics

1. **Put the most extraordinary thing as close to the start as possible.** *Proof*
   your video is special beats a *promise* it's special. Most creators save the
   best for last ("watch to the end") and only ~20% get there. Grab attention with
   the single most attention-grabbing thing you can offer, up front (classic Mr
   Beast cold-opens).
2. **Infectious enthusiasm.** Raw enthusiasm convinces the viewer you have
   something genuinely special, so they're curious and they trust you — even if
   you're unknown ("How to make pickle Pepsi at home" → 8M views largely on
   enthusiasm). Whatever emotion you put in your voice is contagious.
3. **Never let the viewer feel they know enough about the subject.** Before
   explaining something, tell them *what they're missing by not knowing it.* Keep
   a cadence of fact → question → next fact (Vsauce) so they never feel complete.
4. **Introduce an antagonist / constant threat of failure.** An opponent creates
   ongoing dramatic conflict (Dream's Minecraft Manhunt: an underdog to root for,
   competition, imposing challenges, creative escapes).
5. **Make the low-conflict parts go by as fast as possible.** Summarize the setup
   (Mark Rober compresses a year of planning + build into a ~20–30s montage) so
   the video reaches the exciting part ASAP.
6. **Open on the most broadly appealing question.** Start with the question on
   everyone's mind ("What is using the Apple Vision Pro actually *like*?"), then
   layer the fascinating details afterward.
7. **Name a common pain point before solving it.** Prove you understand the
   viewer's problem (Johnny Harris: "you're probably bored too… let me help"). Do
   this throughout, not just the intro — it makes as many people as possible care.
8. **Introduce a time limit** → urgency + threat of failure (Mr Beast's "5 minutes
   before the meteor hits"). Turns "doing something" into a thrilling challenge.
9. **Develop a mystery → curiosity.** Plant something unexplained and *revisit it
   later* (Ryan's mysterious room with giant paw prints, brought back 6 minutes
   on). Leaving early means leaving with unanswered questions. Great for hooks too.
10. **Share the stakes → drama.** Raise what's at risk and *why it matters to this
    specific person* (Mr Beast asks contestants why winning matters to them). You
    don't need money — a bet with a funny forfeit works (Ryan).
11. **When it gets stale, introduce a new challenge/objective.** Add a fresh goal
    mid-video ("build a community and throw the best party on the cruise ship") to
    give something new to look forward to that could also fail.
12. **Enhance the natural tone of each moment with editing.** Match music, clip
    duration, and sound design to the underlying emotion so the music always
    represents the mood of that exact moment — for stories *and* explainers.
13. **Give something to look forward to + clear progression toward it.** Start
    with the lamest part and escalate (Ryan: cheapest room → progressively better
    rooms), or a top-10, or increasing difficulty, or any clear goal the video
    visibly advances toward.
14. **Share what might go wrong → suspense + curiosity.** Constantly tell the
    viewer what would happen if things failed and every reason they could fail
    (freezing water, a life vest that "gives you 1 hour to live"). Maximizes fear
    and uncertainty → viewers on the edge of their seat.
15. **Maintain perfect clarity so the viewer never gets confused.** Step in with a
    voiceover to clarify where you are in the story (and spark curiosity at the
    same time). Immersion requires clarity at all times.
16. **Introduce competition** → drama, curiosity, excitement. People vs people,
    teams, or even food vs food (Joshua Weissman ranking every state's breakfast).
    You're curious who wins and there's conflict driving each scene.
17. **Highlight something counterintuitive or surprising.** Seek out the
    unexpected ("we can predict eclipses millennia out but not the wind in an
    hour") — it always warrants an explanation. The best conversation-starters get
    people curious.
18. **Create a highlight reel of the best moments/info.** Package a months-long,
    multi-country story into a tight ~9-minute emotional roller coaster. Doesn't
    take budget — "I Did A Thing" out-viewed a huge-budget video by carving a
    wooden spoon with clever application of these tactics (13M views).

## How to use this in a critique

- Walk the draft's timeline and, at each stretch, ask **"what would the viewer
  miss by leaving *right here*?"** If the answer is unclear, that's a drop point.
- For each drop point, prescribe a *specific* tactic by number ("plant a mystery
  at 2:10 and pay it off at 6:00" = #9; "compress this setup" = #5).
- Confusion is the silent killer — check #15 first (clarity) before adding intrigue.
- Retention and entertainment overlap heavily; pair this with `entertainment.md`
  (the green/purple reason-to-watch formula) and `editing.md` (tactic #12's
  execution).
