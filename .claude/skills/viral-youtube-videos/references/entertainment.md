# Entertainment & storytelling — the 6 components of an addictive video

> Distilled from 8 years of writing scripts, editing, and investigating what goes
> viral. These six components are what make a video's retention so high it's
> "guaranteed" to go viral. They work for **narrative *and* explainer** content.

---

## 1. Give every part a reason to watch — the green/purple formula

Stop trimming your video down to only "the parts your audience wants." Value the
viewer doesn't *know* is coming is just boring information. Before every payoff,
create curiosity or an expectation.

- **Green sections = the reason to watch.** State a problem, share a mystery, pose
  a question, or set an expectation.
- **Purple sections = the payoff.** Reveal the answer/lesson/result.
- Color-code your script green/purple and make sure the viewer *always* cares so
  much about what's coming that they can't stop watching. This formula is
  everywhere (intros are green; "problem → solution → problem → solution";
  "question → answer → question → answer").

**The 4 ways to create curiosity (a green section):**
1. **A mystery / problem / question / obstacle** that lacks an answer.
2. **Something counterintuitive or surprising.**
3. **Someone knows a secret** — imply something important is being hidden.
4. **A missing component** — something important not yet considered.

**Key law: intrigue comes from uncertainty.** Ryan's journey to the "world's
loneliest house" is made as interesting as the house itself by stressing it's
*dangerous* and he's *unprepared* — his success becomes uncertain.

**Ryan's 3-part litmus test** for what to keep vs cut vs voice-over. The viewer
must always be told:
1. **What's happening**, 2. **Why it's important**, 3. **What could go wrong.**
Tell all three and they'll watch whatever follows. Say it live if you can; if you
forgot on camera, add it as a **voiceover in the edit.**

**The cut rule:** if you *can't* create curiosity or an expectation for a part
(no chance of failure, nothing to wonder about), **cut it — or compress it to a
sentence or two of voiceover.** Don't show footage that has no reason to be watched.

## 2. Bring the right energy on camera

Monotone makes anything boring; **whatever emotion you put in your voice is
contagious** — if you're uncomfortable, the viewer feels uncomfortable.

- Speak **louder for excitement, lower for suspense, pause to build
  anticipation.** Raise eyebrows, squint on a key detail, move your head with your
  words, use your hands to separate ideas / show scale.
- Even Vsauce reading colors off a Rubik's cube is engaging because he *sounds and
  looks like he's saying something important* (30M-view intro).
- **The camera-shy fix:** you talk fine to friends because 100% of your focus is
  on getting the point across. On camera you turn self-conscious ("am I talking
  fast enough?"), so you memorize words — which makes it *worse*, because now
  you're focused on the words, not the meaning → monotone.
- **The real fix: get comfortable making mistakes.** An excited take with a few
  "um"s (cut later) beats a flawless monotone one. Treat the camera like a friend
  who's genuinely interested — connect with the *individual* viewer, don't
  "perform" for an imagined panel of critics.

---

## 3. Use an addictive structure — "but / therefore," not "and then"

The South Park rule. A story told as "this happened **and then** this **and
then** this" gets repetitive *and* never specifies how one thing leads to the
next — disorienting, with lots of exit points to disengage.

Instead: **"We tried this, BUT that got in the way, THEREFORE we had to do this,
BUT it didn't work, SO we tried this."** Apply it to scenes in a story, topics in
an explanation, even sentences in a paragraph. This creates a **seamless flow**
that keeps the viewer immersed start to finish.

---

## 4. Communicate value better — kill confusion

The **biggest** retention mistake, found by auditing real feedback, is **confusion
— "I was confused what was happening"** — and almost no one thinks they do it.

- **You are biased.** You have all the context (you did the research, filming,
  editing). The viewer comes from **zero context** — everything is new, so it's
  much harder for them to keep up. This is usually why a high-value video
  underperforms: the value is there, but viewers can't *absorb* it.
- Leo revisits every part of a video **~100 times** to find any way to make it
  easier to understand. Techniques:
  - **Analogies and examples** turn an abstract idea vivid (learning a new video is
    like learning a new game — clear + concise isn't enough until you've *seen* it).
  - **Get someone else to watch before posting.** Every time, they point to a
    moment that was boring or confusing that you were blind to.
  - **Give every single thing you talk about a visual.** Fast pacing makes it easy
    to miss details, so use visual communication to deliver max value at max
    efficiency — and **zoom/pan to exactly where the viewer should be looking**,
    since a scene often has several things competing for the eye.

---

## 5. Add variety — but like seasoning, not a dump

**Variety is like seasoning in a dish: it brings out the flavor, but too much
ruins it.** Adding variety *just to have it* can wreck a video.

- **Mood variety = great.** A constant tone is one seasoning — boring. Let mood
  fluctuate: excitement → worry about a threat → joy at a solution → unease at the
  next obstacle. The shifting tone keeps the viewer alert and the contrast makes
  each mood hit harder.
- **Visual variety = handle with care.** Too much and the viewer isn't given time
  to process each thing on screen, so they absorb nothing. Think about the *effect*
  each element has and *how much* is actually needed — don't toss in all the spices.

---

## 6. Study your niche and out-deliver

The algorithm recommends the video predicted to be *most enjoyable* to a person;
make it enjoyable to *millions* and it's recommended to millions.

- Take an audience you **understand** (you know what they want from YouTube), then
  figure out **exactly what they want and how to give it better than the
  competition.**
- Concretely: look at the **most-viewed videos in your niche**, compare them to
  yours, and keep noticing + improving the things they do better than you until
  you match or beat their views. That's the whole loop — study what got the most
  views, learn what the audience values, deliver that value better than anyone.
- You don't need "crazy YouTuber" stunts — you need to *present* things more
  entertainingly using these methods (a guy spending two days in a house with the
  lights off became a captivating story purely through framing).
